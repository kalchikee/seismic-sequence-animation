# Geological Interpretation — 2019 Ridgecrest Sequence

## Tectonic Setting

The 2019 Ridgecrest earthquake sequence occurred within the Eastern California Shear Zone (ECSZ), a broad zone of distributed right-lateral shear that accommodates ~25% of Pacific–North American plate motion east of the San Andreas Fault. The ECSZ includes the Owens Valley, Garlock, and Ridgecrest fault systems, all of which show evidence of late Quaternary and historical ruptures.

The Ridgecrest region sits at the southern termination of the Owens Valley fault system and the northern margin of the Mojave Desert. Pre-existing northwest-striking right-lateral faults and northeast-striking left-lateral faults form a conjugate system that accommodates the NW-directed shear in this part of the plate boundary. The 2019 sequence activated both fault orientations simultaneously, with the Mw6.4 foreshock rupturing a NE-striking left-lateral fault and the Mw7.1 mainshock rupturing a NW-striking right-lateral fault — a textbook conjugate fault pair geometry.

## Mainshock Rupture

The July 6, 2019 Mw7.1 mainshock ruptured ~45 km of the NW-striking (strike 322°) Airport Lake fault zone. The surface rupture trace extended from near the town of Ridgecrest to the southern Coso Range, with a maximum right-lateral offset of ~3.5 m near the Searles Valley. Slip on the fault plane was concentrated between 0 and 12 km depth (the seismogenic layer), with maximum slip at ~5 km depth.

InSAR observations revealed a 4-lobed coseismic deformation pattern consistent with right-lateral slip, with surface displacements exceeding 1.5 m LOS along the fault. The conjugate fault pair geometry meant that the fault plane was loaded by the foreshock stress change in a manner that promoted Coulomb failure, consistent with the <36-hour foreshock-mainshock interval.

## Post-Seismic Deformation

The Ridgecrest sequence produced one of the most well-documented post-seismic deformation sequences in the modern geodetic era. Three mechanisms contribute:

1. **Afterslip** (dominant, 0–3 months): Shallow fault creep extending the coseismic rupture at the surface and at the base of the seismogenic zone. Characterized by rapid early velocity that decays logarithmically.

2. **Viscoelastic relaxation** (secondary, 3+ months): Distributed viscous flow in the lower crust and upper mantle accommodating the stress step from the mainshock. Produces a broad, slow displacement field extending tens of km from the fault.

3. **Poroelastic rebound** (minor): Pore pressure re-equilibration in the upper crust driven by coseismic volumetric strain. Amplitude typically <5% of total post-seismic signal.

## Aftershock Sequence Interpretation

The aftershock distribution illuminates fault structures that were not visible at the surface. Several key observations emerge:

**Conjugate reactivation:** Aftershocks cluster on both NW-striking and NE-striking planes, reflecting reactivation of the pre-existing conjugate fault network throughout the seismogenic zone. This is consistent with the regional stress field orientation (SHmax ~N–S) that promotes both fault orientations under right-lateral transtension.

**Depth distribution:** The aftershock distribution deepens to the northwest, consistent with cooler, stronger crust in that direction. The base of the seismogenic zone (~15 km) represents the depth at which temperature exceeds ~350°C and quartz plasticity becomes the dominant deformation mechanism.

**B-value anomaly:** The low-b region (b ≈ 0.7) at the junction of the conjugate faults likely reflects a locked, mechanically competent asperity where differential stress is highest — consistent with the location of the M7.1 hypocenter.

**Migration pattern:** The ~8 km/day NW migration of seismicity in the first 72 hours is consistent with afterslip front propagation rather than fluid diffusion (which would produce √t-dependent migration at rates of ~1–2 km/day). This supports a frictional afterslip model rather than a pore pressure triggering model for the early post-seismic sequence.

## Implications for Regional Hazard

The Ridgecrest sequence demonstrated that significant M7+ earthquakes can occur on unmapped or poorly characterized fault systems within the ECSZ, at distances of >100 km from the San Andreas Fault proper. The sequence contributed new slip to multiple fault strands, potentially increasing Coulomb stress on adjacent segments of the Garlock Fault (which runs ~30 km to the south), a major M7.5–8.0 capable structure.
